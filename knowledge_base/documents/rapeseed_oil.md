# Rapeseed Oil

## Summary
Rapeseed oil (often called canola oil in North America) is a widely used vegetable oil pressed from the seeds of the rapeseed plant. It is popular for cooking and food manufacturing because it is mild in flavour, relatively heat-stable, and rich in unsaturated fats, especially monounsaturated fats and omega-3 fatty acids.

## Health Risks
- Like all oils, rapeseed oil is energy-dense, so large amounts can contribute to excess calorie intake and weight gain if not used in moderation.
- Highly refined rapeseed oil used in many ultra-processed foods may contain fewer beneficial micronutrients and is often consumed alongside salt, sugar, and refined carbohydrates, which can negatively affect health when eaten in excess.
- Rapeseed oil is relatively high in omega-6 fatty acids; if overall dietary intake of omega-3 is low and omega-6 intake is very high, this imbalance may contribute to a more pro-inflammatory diet pattern.
- People with fat-malabsorption conditions or on medically prescribed low-fat diets may need to limit all added fats, including rapeseed oil, according to clinical advice.

## Health Benefits
- Rapeseed oil is low in saturated fat and high in unsaturated fats, which can help support healthy blood cholesterol levels when it replaces fats high in saturated fat.
- It provides alpha-linolenic acid (ALA), a plant omega-3 fatty acid that contributes to heart and vascular health as part of an overall balanced diet.
- Rapeseed oil contains vitamin E and other antioxidant compounds that help protect cells from oxidative damage.
- Compared with some other common cooking oils, its favourable balance of monounsaturated and polyunsaturated fats makes it a heart-friendly choice when used in moderate amounts.

## Healthier Alternatives
- Extra-virgin olive oil offers similar heart benefits with a higher content of polyphenols, especially when used unheated in salads and dressings.
- Oils such as flaxseed (linseed) oil and walnut oil provide higher amounts of omega-3 ALA but are best used cold rather than for high-heat cooking.
- For many people, the biggest improvement comes from reducing total added fat, choosing less processed foods, and using small amounts of rapeseed or olive oil instead of butter, lard, or tropical oils high in saturated fat.

## Allergen Info
- Rapeseed oil is not considered a common food allergen, and refined edible rapeseed oil typically contains negligible intact protein.
- People with rare rapeseed or mustard seed allergies may wish to discuss rapeseed oil with an allergy specialist, particularly if using cold-pressed, minimally refined oils that may contain more residual seed material.

## Nutritional Snapshot (per 100g)
- Calories: ~900 kcal (typical for vegetable oils)
- Fat: ~100 g total fat, of which the majority is monounsaturated and polyunsaturated fat, with relatively low saturated fat
