# BHA_BHT (E320, E321)

## Summary
BHA (butylated hydroxyanisole, E320) and BHT (butylated hydroxytoluene, E321) are synthetic antioxidants added to foods and fats to prevent spoilage and rancidity. They are commonly used in processed foods, oils, snack products, and also in animal feed, cosmetics, and packaging materials.

## Health Risks
- High doses of BHA cause tumors in the forestomach of rodents, leading to its classification as having sufficient evidence of carcinogenicity in animals, though relevance to typical human dietary exposure remains uncertain.
- BHA and BHT can cause organ-specific effects in animal studies, including impacts on the liver, kidney, thyroid, and urinary bladder at high experimental doses.
- EFSA evaluations have set acceptable daily intakes (ADIs) for both BHA and BHT based on long-term toxicity, reproductive, and thyroid effects seen in animals.
- BHA is considered a skin and eye irritant and a potential skin sensitiser for workers handling concentrated forms; inhalation exposure is generally considered low in typical food use.
- For some population groups (especially children with high intake of processed foods), estimated exposure to BHT can approach or exceed the ADI at high-percentile consumption in certain European countries.

## Health Benefits
- BHA and BHT extend shelf life of fats and fat-containing foods by preventing oxidation, which can reduce formation of rancid off-flavours and some oxidation products.
- By stabilising fats, these antioxidants can help maintain the energy value of foods over longer storage times.
- Their use can reduce food waste by slowing spoilage in processed foods and animal feeds.

## Healthier Alternatives
- Using more stable fats and oils (e.g., high-oleic vegetable oils) that are naturally less prone to oxidation.
- Natural antioxidants such as mixed tocopherols (vitamin E) and rosemary extract, where technologically suitable and permitted.
- Reformulating products to reduce overall fat content and reliance on long shelf-life ultra-processed foods.

## Allergen Info
- BHA and BHT are not recognised as major allergens and are not part of the EU top-14 allergen list.
- Most people tolerate these additives, but sensitive individuals may prefer to avoid them due to non-specific intolerance symptoms (e.g., headaches or skin irritation) sometimes reported anecdotally.

## Nutritional Snapshot (per 100g)
- Calories: negligible in practice (used in very small amounts, typically a few mg per kg of food)
- Main function: antioxidant to protect fats and oils from oxidation and rancidity
