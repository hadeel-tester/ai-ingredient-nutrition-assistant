# Artificial Flavours

## Summary
Artificial flavours are chemically defined substances added to foods to impart or modify taste and aroma, and they represent the most commonly used class of food additives worldwide.  Each individual flavouring substance must undergo a structured safety assessment (including exposure estimates and toxicology) before approval, and many have been judged to present no appreciable health risk at authorised use levels. [iris.who](https://iris.who.int/bitstream/handle/10665/42849/WHO_TRS_922.pdf?sequence=1)

## Health Risks
- Safety evaluations focus on chemical identity, purity (often ≥95% of the named compound), metabolism, and toxicological profile, with particular attention to genotoxicity; substances that raise genotoxic concern can be restricted or removed from the market. [efsa.europa](https://www.efsa.europa.eu/en/news/flavouring-substance-considered-safety-concern)
- While most approved flavouring substances are considered low risk at typical intakes, some individual flavourings have raised concerns (for example, perilla aldehyde was identified as potentially genotoxic), showing that not all flavourings are automatically “safe” and that ongoing re-evaluation is important. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC7008796/)
- Mixtures of additives including certain colours and preservatives have been linked to small increases in hyperactive behaviour in some children, and although flavourings were not the primary focus, this has led to broader caution around artificial additives in sensitive individuals. [cordis.europa](https://cordis.europa.eu/article/id/28309-study-links-food-colourings-to-hyperactive-behaviours)

## Health Benefits
- Artificial flavours help restore or standardise taste in processed foods when natural flavours are lost during manufacturing or storage, supporting consistent product quality. [who](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/food-additives)
- Because they are potent and used in tiny amounts, artificial flavourings can enhance palatability without significantly affecting calories, macronutrients, or the overall nutritional profile of the food. [iris.who](https://iris.who.int/bitstream/handle/10665/42849/WHO_TRS_922.pdf?sequence=1)

## Healthier Alternatives
- Natural flavourings and flavouring complexes (for example, extracts and essential oils) can sometimes replace artificial counterparts, provided they also meet safety specifications and purity criteria. [inchem](https://inchem.org/documents/jecfa/jecmono/v52je19.htm)
- Reformulating products to rely more on whole-food ingredients (such as herbs, spices, fruits, and fermentation-derived flavours) can reduce dependence on added artificial flavourings while still providing appealing taste. [inchem](https://inchem.org/documents/jecfa/jecmono/v52je19.htm)

## Allergen Info
- Most authorised flavouring substances are not major IgE-mediated allergens, and flavourings as a general class are not listed among the main regulated food allergens. [efsa.europa](https://www.efsa.europa.eu/en/topics/topic/flavourings)
- However, complex flavour mixtures or natural flavouring complexes can contain trace constituents capable of provoking reactions in susceptible individuals, so people with known sensitivities are often advised to monitor symptoms with highly flavoured processed foods. [efsa.europa](https://www.efsa.europa.eu/en/topics/topic/flavourings)

## Nutritional Snapshot (per 100g)
- Calories: variable but typically low, because flavourings are used at very small concentrations and contribute negligible energy to the finished product. [who](https://www.who.int/news-room/fact-sheets/detail/food-additives)
- Use levels are usually in the range of parts per million in foods, and regulatory risk assessments rely on detailed exposure estimates to ensure that even high consumers remain within safe intake margins. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36579172/)
