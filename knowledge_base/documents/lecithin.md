# Lecithin (E322)

## Summary
Lecithin is a mixture of phospholipids extracted from soybeans, sunflower seeds, rapeseed, or eggs, functioning as a natural emulsifier that blends oil and water in chocolate, margarine, mayonnaise, and baked goods. It improves texture, prevents separation, extends shelf life, and reduces viscosity while providing choline essential for cell membranes and liver function. GRAS status by FDA and approved by EFSA with no numerical ADI due to low toxicity.

## Health Risks
- Soy-derived versions may trigger reactions in highly soy-allergic individuals despite minimal protein content.
- Large supplemental doses cause gastrointestinal upset (diarrhea, nausea, bloating).
- Weight gain possible from calorie-dense nature when overconsumed as supplement.
- Estrogen-like effects theoretically possible from soy phytoestrogens but not demonstrated at food levels.

## Health Benefits
- Rich source of choline supporting brain function, memory, and liver fat metabolism.
- Clinical studies show cholesterol reduction (LDL down 40-50% in some trials).
- Emulsifying properties may improve fat digestion and nutrient absorption.
- Antioxidant protection from phosphatidylcholine preserving cell membranes.

## Healthier Alternatives
- Sunflower lecithin (allergen-free alternative to soy).
- Minimal processing with simple ingredient lists.
- Natural food combinations providing similar emulsifying fats.

## Allergen Info
- Not a top-14 EU allergen itself.
- Soy lecithin requires allergen declaration; sunflower versions allergen-free.
- Egg lecithin unsuitable for egg-allergic consumers.

## Nutritional Snapshot (per 100g)
- Calories: 700 kcal
- Fat: 65g (phospholipids)
- Choline: 20-30g
- Carbohydrates/protein: minimal
