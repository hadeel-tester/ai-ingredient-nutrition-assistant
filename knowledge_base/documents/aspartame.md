# Aspartame (E951)

## Summary
Aspartame is an artificial sweetener around 200 times sweeter than sugar, widely used in diet soft drinks, sugar-free foods, and chewing gum. It provides sweetness with very few calories because only small amounts are needed. In 2023, the International Agency for Research on Cancer (IARC) classified aspartame as “possibly carcinogenic to humans” (Group 2B), while WHO/JECFA and EFSA continue to consider it safe at current acceptable daily intake (ADI) levels.

## Health Risks
- IARC’s Group 2B classification was based on limited evidence for liver cancer (hepatocellular carcinoma) in humans, limited evidence in experimental animals, and limited mechanistic evidence.
- WHO/JECFA’s 2023 assessment reaffirmed the ADI of 0–40 mg/kg body weight per day, concluding that available data do not provide convincing evidence of harm at or below this intake.
- EFSA’s 2013 full re-evaluation also concluded that aspartame and its breakdown products are safe for the general population at current exposure levels, including children and pregnant women.
- Aspartame breaks down in the gut to phenylalanine, aspartic acid, and methanol; for people with phenylketonuria (PKU), the phenylalanine component can be dangerous and must be strictly avoided.
- Some individuals report headaches, mood changes, or other non-specific symptoms after consuming aspartame-containing products, but controlled studies have not consistently confirmed a causal link.

## Health Benefits
- Aspartame provides sweetness with negligible calories, which can help reduce energy intake when used to replace sugar in foods and drinks.
- It does not raise blood glucose in the same way as sugar, making it an option for people with diabetes when used as part of an overall dietary plan.
- Because it does not promote tooth decay, aspartame-containing sugar-free products can help reduce risk of dental caries compared with sugary alternatives.

## Healthier Alternatives
- Stevia-based sweeteners (from steviol glycosides) provide intense sweetness with a strong safety record when consumed within their ADI.
- Sugar alcohols such as erythritol or xylitol can provide bulk and sweetness with fewer calories than sugar and minimal impact on blood glucose, though large amounts may cause digestive discomfort.
- Gradually reducing overall sweetness in the diet (less reliance on both sugar and high-intensity sweeteners) can help reset taste preferences and lower long-term sugar and sweetener intake.

## Allergen Info
- Aspartame is not a recognised major allergen and is not on the EU top-14 allergen list.
- EU regulations require that foods containing aspartame, or aspartame–acesulfame salt, are labelled to indicate that they contain a source of phenylalanine so that people with PKU can avoid them.

## Nutritional Snapshot (per 100g)
- Calories: ~400 kcal per 100 g as a pure powder, but typical serving quantities in foods and drinks are tiny, contributing only a few kilocalories per portion.
- Sweetness: ~200 times sweeter than sucrose, so much smaller amounts are needed to achieve the same sweetness.
