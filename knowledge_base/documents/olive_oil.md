# Olive Oil

## Summary
Olive oil is a liquid fat obtained from olives, widely used for cooking and as a key component of the Mediterranean diet. It is particularly rich in monounsaturated fat (mainly oleic acid) and contains bioactive compounds such as polyphenols, which have been linked to cardiovascular and anti-inflammatory benefits. [frontiersin](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2022.1041203/full)

## Health Risks
- Olive oil is energy-dense, so excessive use can contribute to weight gain if total calorie intake is not controlled. [healthline](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/extra-virgin-olive-oil)
- Refined olive oils contain fewer polyphenols and antioxidants than extra virgin olive oil, potentially offering less cardiometabolic benefit. [healthline](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/extra-virgin-olive-oil)
- Very high-heat cooking can degrade some beneficial compounds and generate oxidation products, especially if oil is overheated or reused many times. [healthline](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/extra-virgin-olive-oil)
- People on medically prescribed low-fat diets or with specific fat-malabsorption disorders may need to limit all added fats, including olive oil, under medical supervision. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36343558/)

## Health Benefits
- Extra virgin olive oil is high in monounsaturated fat and has been associated with reduced risk of cardiovascular disease and all-cause mortality in large prospective studies and meta-analyses. [pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4030221/)
- It provides vitamin E, vitamin K, and numerous polyphenols with antioxidant and anti-inflammatory effects that may help protect blood vessels and improve lipid profiles. [healthline](https://www.healthline.com/nutrition/extra-virgin-olive-oil)
- Higher olive oil intake within a Mediterranean-style dietary pattern has been linked to lower rates of heart attack, stroke, and cardiovascular death in high-risk individuals. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40907633/)
- Replacing saturated-fat-rich fats (like butter or lard) with olive oil can help lower LDL cholesterol and improve overall cardiometabolic risk markers. [frontiersin](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2022.1041203/full)

## Healthier Alternatives
- Extra virgin olive oil itself is often considered a healthier choice among added fats, especially when it replaces fats high in saturated or trans fats. [frontiersin](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2022.1041203/full)
- For additional omega-3 ALA, small amounts of flaxseed or walnut oil (used unheated) can complement olive oil in a varied diet. [pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/36343558/)
- For many people, the main improvement is reducing total added fat and prioritising whole foods (nuts, seeds, avocado, olives) alongside modest amounts of extra virgin olive oil instead of highly processed fats. [frontiersin](https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/nutrition/articles/10.3389/fnut.2022.1041203/full)

## Allergen Info
- Olive oil is not a common food allergen, and refined forms contain very little olive protein.
- Rarely, individuals with olive or olive pollen allergy may experience symptoms and should seek personalised medical advice if concerned.

## Nutritional Snapshot (per 100g)
- Calories: ~884 kcal (about 119 kcal per tablespoon). [petrafoshotel](https://www.petrafoshotel.com/the-nutritional-value-of-extra-virgin-olive-oil/)
- Fat: ~100 g total fat, predominantly monounsaturated (around 70–75%), with smaller amounts of saturated and polyunsaturated fats; also provides vitamin E and vitamin K in meaningful amounts. [petrafoshotel](https://www.petrafoshotel.com/the-nutritional-value-of-extra-virgin-olive-oil/)
