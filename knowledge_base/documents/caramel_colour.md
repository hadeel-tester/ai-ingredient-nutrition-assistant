# Caramel Colour (E150a–E150d)

## Summary
Caramel colour is a widely used brown food colouring produced by controlled heating of carbohydrates, often in the presence of acids, alkalis, or salts. It appears in soft drinks, sauces, baked goods, confectionery, and many processed foods to provide a uniform brown appearance. Regulatory bodies recognise four main classes (E150a–E150d), which differ in their manufacturing process and potential by-products such as 4-methylimidazole (4-MI) and THI (2-acetyl-4-tetrahydroxybutylimidazole).

## Health Risks
- EFSA has established a group acceptable daily intake (ADI) for the four caramel colours based on toxicological data, with a lower contribution allowed from Class III caramel (E150c) due to immune-system concerns related to THI.
- For E150c and E150d, by-products like 4-MI and THI have raised toxicological concerns in animal studies, including effects on blood cells and potential carcinogenicity, but typical dietary exposures in Europe were concluded to be below levels of concern in refined exposure assessments.
- WHO/JECFA evaluations of caramel colours, particularly ammoniated classes, have highlighted lymphocyte and leucocyte depression as key effects at high doses, leading to earlier temporary ADIs and later revisions as specification limits for contaminants were tightened.
- Monitoring studies show that 4-MI and THI can be present in caramel colour and caramel-coloured foods, but measured levels in commercial products are generally within regulatory limits set to protect consumers.

## Health Benefits
- Caramel colour has no direct nutritional benefit, but it supports product stability and consumer acceptance by giving a consistent colour to foods and beverages.
- Its use can help standardise the appearance of products made from variable raw materials, which can indirectly reduce food waste linked to visual quality issues.

## Healthier Alternatives
- Using less intensely coloured products or accepting natural colour variation can reduce the need for added colourings.
- Alternative colour ingredients such as certain plant extracts may be used in some applications, though they may not fully replicate the stability and shade of caramel colour.
- Choosing products with minimal additives overall (e.g., water, unsweetened teas, and less processed foods) reduces exposure to caramel colour and similar additives.

## Allergen Info
- Caramel colours E150a–E150d are not recognised as major allergens and are not on the EU top-14 allergen list.
- Although hypersensitivity reactions are rare, sensitive individuals who suspect intolerance to caramel-coloured soft drinks or sauces may choose to limit intake.

## Nutritional Snapshot (per 100g)
- Calories: very low in typical use (added at small concentrations mainly for colouring, not as a nutrient source)
- Main function: colour additive providing brown to dark brown tones in beverages and processed foods
